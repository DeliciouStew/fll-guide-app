Section 02 · Chassis

Build a stable base

A good chassis doesn't just hold the robot together — it makes every mission more consistent. Get the foundation right first.

⚙️
Drive System
How the robot moves
▼
  • Use a differential drive (2 driven wheels + 1 or 2 passive casters)
  • Place the two drive wheels as far apart as possible for stable turning
  • Use the same size wheels on both sides — mismatched wheels cause drift
  • Keep caster wheels lightweight so they don't affect the drive
💡 For SPIKE Prime: Large wheels on ports A & E give the most torque and speed balance.
⚖️
Weight & Balance
Stability under movement
▼
  • Keep the center of gravity low and centered between the drive wheels
  • Place the hub/brick as low as possible on the chassis
  • Heavy attachments at the front can cause the robot to "nose-dive" — counterbalance with rear weight
  • Test by pushing gently on each side — it should not tip easily
💡 A robot that tips during a run loses the mission. Balance first, then speed.
📐
Straight Driving
The most critical skill
▼
  • A symmetric chassis is essential — measure distances carefully when building
  • Both drive axles must be perfectly parallel
  • Use a gyro sensor to correct drift during long straight runs
  • Test by driving 1 meter with no steering — it should deviate less than 2 cm
  • Tighten all axle joints — loose parts cause inconsistency
💡 Build the chassis on a flat surface and check symmetry with a ruler before adding anything else.
🔩
Structural Rigidity
No flex, no wobble
▼
  • Add cross-beams to prevent the frame from twisting
  • Use pins and bushings to lock all rotating parts that should be fixed
  • Shake the robot gently — nothing should rattle or shift
  • Reinforce the attachment mounting point — this takes the most stress
💡 A flex in the frame translates directly into inconsistency. Build rigid, test rigid.
🏁
Launch Position
Same spot every run
▼
  • Design a flat, flush base — the robot must sit perfectly on the home base line
  • Add a small notch or alignment guide that touches the mat wall/line
  • Mark the exact starting orientation with tape during practice
  • Every team member must place the robot the same way — practice this too
💡 Even a 2mm offset at launch can mean missing the target 60 cm away.
🛞
Wheel Grip
Traction on the mat
▼
  • Use rubber-grip tires, not smooth plastic wheels
  • Keep tire surfaces clean — grease or dust reduces grip drastically
  • Wipe tires with a dry cloth before every match
  • Avoid pressing the robot down hard on the mat — caster should barely touch
💡 Check tire cleanliness during setup — it's one of the easiest consistency wins.

Section 02 · Attachments

Design mission tools

Attachments are the robot's hands. Each one should do one job and do it reliably — every single time.

🔄
Quick-Connect System
Swap tools between runs
▼
  • Design one shared mounting point on the chassis that all attachments connect to
  • Use a consistent connector — same pin holes, same orientation every time
  • Attachment must lock with a satisfying "click" — no wobble when mounted
  • Practice swapping under time pressure: target under 5 seconds
💡 A loose attachment shifts during movement and ruins precision. Test by shaking the robot after connecting.
↔️
Push Attachments
Simple and reliable
▼
  • Best for missions where the robot drives into an element to trigger it
  • Design the push surface to be wider than the target — more room for error
  • Angle the pusher slightly downward so it slides under elements rather than pushing them up
  • Works well for: M05 Who Lived Here?, M13 Statue Rebuild
💡 Push attachments have zero moving parts — they never fail mechanically. Use them when possible.
⬆️
Lift Attachments
Raise or flip elements
▼
  • Use a motor-driven arm or a passive lever that activates on contact
  • Keep the range of motion simple: 0° to 90° is usually enough
  • Motor lift: program to a fixed rotation count, not time (more consistent)
  • Works well for: M09 What's on Sale?, M12 Salvage Operation
💡 Avoid rubber bands for tension — they degrade over many runs. Use gear ratios for consistent force instead.
🪝
Hook & Grab
Pick up or pull elements
▼
  • Design the hook to slide over the target from above — don't approach from the side
  • The opening must be slightly larger than the target element for easy engagement
  • Add a guide rail so the robot doesn't need to be perfectly aligned to hook
  • Works well for: M07 Heavy Lifting, M04 Careful Recovery
💡 Test with the element in the worst-case position, not the ideal one. If it works in the worst case, you're good.
🎯
Alignment Guides
Remove the need for precision
▼
  • Add "funnel" guides on the attachment that steer it onto the target even if slightly off
  • Use mat lines or mission model surfaces to align before engaging
  • A wider contact surface tolerates more robot drift
  • Think: "what happens if we're 1 cm left?" — the design should still work
💡 The best attachment design makes it nearly impossible to miss, even with imperfect driving.
🔁
Repeatability Testing
Run it 20 times, not once
▼
  • Run each mission at least 20 times before considering it "done"
  • Track success rate — aim for 90%+ before competition
  • If it fails, note the exact failure mode and fix the root cause
  • Test with different team members placing the robot — human variation is real
  • Reset the mission model to exact starting position each time
💡 One successful run means nothing. Twenty successful runs in a row means you're ready.
